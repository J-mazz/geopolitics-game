I. Prologue

What follows is not opinion. It is not partisan. It belongs to no faction.

It is a compilation of verifiable public record—court filings, government documents, published policy proposals, confirmed appointments, and reporting from established news organizations—about the architecture of executive power consolidation in the United States as of February 2026.

The record speaks for itself. Every factual claim is documented with specific evidence. The reader is urged to verify independently. This document was written for every citizen of the republic—regardless of party, background, or belief—because the Constitution belongs to all of us.

Scoring Rubric

▼ Expand

Each indicator is evaluated on a 0–10 scale measuring institutional health and constraint on executive power. This rubric applies consistently across all signal types.

0–2: Open Defiance / Non-Function
Institution refuses to operate or explicitly defies legal authority; complete breakdown of checks; norm obliterated beyond recognition.
3–4: Persistent Impairment
Institution operating in degraded state with serious structural damage; independence compromised; oversight mechanisms failing; norms under sustained attack.
5–6: Strained but Operative
Institution functioning under stress; independence contested but not eliminated; checks delayed or weakened but not destroyed; norms violated but not abandoned.
7–8: Robust
Institution operating with meaningful independence; checks and balances largely effective; occasional norm violations met with resistance; structural integrity maintained.
9–10: Exemplary
Institution demonstrating exceptional independence and accountability; checks operating effectively; norms reinforced through practice; system functioning as designed by framers.

Note: Evaluation scores reflect institutional health relative to constitutional design, not policy outcomes. A score of 3 means structural degradation has occurred, regardless of whether one agrees with the policy direction.

IV. The Mechanism: Unitary Executive Theory

This section presents the constitutional doctrine underlying executive power consolidation, comparing the textual claims with the framers' documented reasoning.

Legal Doctrine 1980s–present
The unitary executive theory, advanced primarily since the 1980s by the Federalist Society and Heritage Foundation, interprets Article II, Section 1 ("The executive Power shall be vested in a President") to mean the President possesses sole and complete control over every function of the executive branch, including the power to override or remove any subordinate.
Evidence
  • "Unitary executive theory," Wikipedia, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_executive_theory
  • "Unitary Executive Theory," Cornell Law School Legal Information Institute overview
  • Lawrence Lessig and Cass R. Sunstein, "The President and the Administration," Columbia Law Review, Vol. 94, No. 1 (1994), pp. 1–123
Interpretation
The strong version of unitary executive theory claims the definite article "the" in "the executive Power" means all executive power without exception. This interpretation supports removing civil service protections (Schedule F), firing independent agency heads (FTC, Fed), and overriding agency expertise with presidential directives. The theory's key mechanism is eliminating the concept of "independent" executive functions—if all executive power belongs to the President personally, no executive officer can claim decisional autonomy. This transforms agencies from institutions with statutory missions into extensions of presidential will.
Constitutional Record 1788
On February 1, 1788, James Madison wrote in Federalist No. 47 that "the accumulation of all powers, legislative, executive, and judiciary, in the same hands, whether of one, a few, or many, and whether hereditary, self-appointed, or elective, may justly be pronounced the very definition of tyranny."
Evidence
  • James Madison, Federalist No. 47 (February 1, 1788), Library of Congress full text
  • The Federalist Papers, Avalon Project, Yale Law School
Interpretation
Madison's formulation explicitly identifies power consolidation—not monarchy specifically, but the accumulation of powers—as tyranny. The phrase "whether hereditary, self-appointed, or elective" forecloses the argument that election legitimizes consolidated power. The framing context: Madison was defending the proposed Constitution against charges that it created an elected monarch. His defense was that powers were separated, not that election made consolidation safe. This directly contradicts the strong unitary executive reading, which seeks to consolidate all executive functions under presidential control. Madison's "very definition of tyranny" applies regardless of whether the consolidating agent is a king or an elected president.
Evaluation
Historical baseline = 9/10 (Exemplary separation). Rationale: This record establishes the framers' intent as exemplary separation of powers. The evaluation isn't of current conditions but of constitutional design. Madison's statement in 1788 documents that power accumulation was the central threat the Constitution was engineered to prevent. Measuring current institutional health requires comparing present consolidation signals against this stated baseline. Federalist 47 was published February 1, 1788, as part of the ratification debate—it's the sales pitch for why separation matters.
Constitutional Record 1788
On March 14, 1788, Alexander Hamilton published Federalist No. 69, systematically distinguishing the proposed president from the British monarch by enumerating specific limitations: limited term vs. life tenure, impeachable vs. inviolable, shared treaty power vs. sole prerogative, and commander-in-chief authority constrained by congressional war and appropriations powers.
Evidence
  • Alexander Hamilton, Federalist No. 69 (March 14, 1788), Library of Congress full text
  • The Federalist Papers, original publication dates, Library of Congress collection
Interpretation
Hamilton's argument structure matters: he was defending the presidency against charges of monarchy by showing how the office was weaker than royal power. The entire essay is a point-by-point limitation list. On military authority, Hamilton writes: "The President is to be commander-in-chief of the army and navy of the United States. In this respect his authority would be nominally the same with that of the king of Great Britain, but in substance much inferior to it." The word "inferior" is not incidental—Hamilton's ratification strategy was convincing Americans the president lacked kingly power. Any constitutional interpretation that yields king-like power contradicts the document's sales pitch to the people who ratified it.
Evaluation
Historical baseline = 9/10 (Exemplary limitation). Rationale: Like Federalist 47, this establishes design intent. Hamilton enumerated limitations on executive power as the reason to ratify. Any doctrine that removes those limitations (e.g., unitary theory enabling presidential override of statutory constraints) operates contrary to the explicit ratification-era understanding. Federalist 69 was published March 14, 1788, five months before New York's ratifying convention—it's a legal commitment by the Constitution's advocates about what the document means.
Textual Evidence 1787 (Constitutional Text)
Article II, Section 2 of the Constitution states the president "may require the Opinion, in writing, of the principal Officer in each of the executive Departments, upon any Subject relating to the Duties of their respective Offices."
Evidence
  • U.S. Constitution, Article II, Section 2, constitution.congress.gov
  • Constitutional Law scholars' analysis (Lessig/Sunstein 1994, others) on the Opinion Clause's implications for unitary executive theory
Interpretation
The Opinion Clause creates a logical problem for strong unitary theory: if the president already possessed total control over executive departments, why would the framers need to specify that he could ask for their opinions? The clause implies department heads have independent judgment that the president must request rather than simply command. This suggests the framers envisioned departments as having some institutional standing apart from presidential diktat. It's a small textual detail but a structural signal—the Constitution grants the president the power to request opinions, not to override statutory duties or eliminate disagreement.
Implementation Signal 2020
On June 29, 2020, the Supreme Court ruled 5–4 in Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau that "the entire 'executive Power' belongs to the President alone," adopting strong unitary executive language into constitutional doctrine.
Evidence
  • Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, 591 U.S. 197 (2020), Supreme Court opinion
  • SCOTUSBlog case analysis, scotusblog.com
  • "Supreme Court ruling on executive power," multiple legal analysis outlets, June 2020
Interpretation
Seila Law represents the Court adopting unitary executive theory into precedent. The phrase "entire executive Power" is maximalist—it forecloses the concept of independent agencies by asserting presidential ownership of all executive functions. The 5–4 split indicates this is contested constitutional ground, not settled doctrine. The implementation mechanism is judicial: once the Court declares a principle, lower courts must follow it, and the executive branch can cite it as authority for removing officials, overriding agency rules, or challenging statutory constraints. This decision creates the legal foundation for consolidating executive power through removal of "for cause" protections.
Evaluation
Score = 5/10 (Strained but Operative). Rationale: The ruling weakens structural independence (lower score) but doesn't eliminate it (not 3-4 range). The 5–4 decision shows institutional division—dissent remains on record, signaling that the doctrine is contested rather than accepted constitutional truth. The Court's adoption of unitary language strains the separation-of-powers architecture because it provides legal cover for executive actions that concentrate power (e.g., firing independent agency heads), but the system remains operative because Congress retains legislative authority and could theoretically pass stronger independence protections. The score reflects the shift in judicial doctrine on June 29, 2020, not the current state of executive power, which is evaluated through implementation signals in other sections.

V. The Instrument: Schedule F / Schedule Policy

This section documents the mechanism for converting career civil service positions into at-will political appointments, removing employment protections and enabling dismissal based on policy disagreement.

Policy Action October 21, 2020
On October 21, 2020, President Trump signed Executive Order 13957 creating "Schedule F," a new employment category reclassifying career federal civil servants in "policy-influencing" positions from competitive service (with employment protections) to at-will status, enabling dismissal without cause.
Evidence
  • Executive Order 13957, October 21, 2020, Federal Register, federalregister.gov
  • "Schedule F," Wikipedia, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schedule_F
  • James Sherk authorship, documented in reporting on EO 13957's origin within Domestic Policy Council
Interpretation
Schedule F's mechanism: search Title 5 USC for existing statutory authority to reclassify positions, then use that authority to convert "policy-influencing" roles (a category broad enough to encompass tens of thousands of positions) from protected to at-will. The brilliance is that it requires no new legislation—it exploits existing law. Career civil service exists to provide institutional memory, expertise, and resistance to unlawful orders across administrations. At-will employment removes that friction. If a career analyst can be fired for refusing to endorse politically desired conclusions, expertise becomes compliance. The order was signed 13 days before the 2020 election—tight enough timing to suggest urgency, late enough to avoid pre-election scrutiny.
Evaluation
Score = 4/10 (Persistent Impairment) if implemented. Rationale: The EO itself is scored based on structural design. Converting protected civil service to at-will political positions crosses into persistent impairment because it removes the institutional check that prevents politically motivated purges. The scoring applies to the potential effect because Biden revoked it two days into his term (January 22, 2021), preventing implementation. But the October 21, 2020 order documents the mechanism's existence and feasibility—it was signed and published in the Federal Register. The question isn't whether Schedule F would degrade institutions (hypothetical) but whether removing civil service protections constitutes degradation (structural). It does. The impairment is in the conversion mechanism itself.
Policy Action January 22, 2021
On January 22, 2021, President Biden revoked Executive Order 13957, eliminating Schedule F before it could be implemented.
Evidence
  • Executive Order revoking EO 13957, January 22, 2021, Federal Register
  • "Biden revokes Trump's Schedule F order," Washington Post, January 22, 2021
  • "Schedule F," Wikipedia timeline
Interpretation
The revocation demonstrates that Schedule F's impact depends on executive will—it can be created or eliminated by presidential order. This reveals both the mechanism's power and its vulnerability. Civil service protections aren't constitutionally mandated; they exist through statute and executive interpretation. An administration committed to consolidating control can reclassify positions; an administration committed to institutional independence can restore protections. The two-day timing (Biden's second day in office) signals that Schedule F was perceived as a urgent threat requiring immediate reversal.
Evaluation
Score = 7/10 (Robust restoration). Rationale: The revocation on January 22, 2021 returned civil service protections to their pre-2020 state, restoring institutional checks. This scores in the robust range because the system proved capable of self-correction through electoral transition. However, it doesn't reach 9-10 (exemplary) because the ease of both creating and revoking Schedule F by executive order alone reveals fragility—civil service independence depends on presidential restraint rather than structural permanence. The mechanism remains available for future use, as demonstrated by 2025 reinstatement (next signal).
Policy Action Early 2025
In early 2025, the second Trump administration reinstated the Schedule F mechanism under the designation "Schedule Policy/Career," reclassifying federal civil service positions to enable at-will dismissal; an updated implementation rule was issued in February 2026.
Evidence
  • "Schedule Policy/Career reinstatement," Federal Register, 2025
  • "Updated reclassification rule," Federal Register, February 2026
  • "Trump reinstates Schedule F as Schedule Policy," Washington Post, Associated Press, Government Executive reporting, 2025
Interpretation
The reinstatement confirms Schedule F wasn't an abandoned idea—it was deferred. The rebrand to "Schedule Policy/Career" suggests lessons learned: softer language ("Policy" rather than "F") may reduce political blowback while retaining the core mechanism. The February 2026 updated rule indicates ongoing refinement, not ad-hoc improvisation. Scale estimates range from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of positions, out of 2.2 million federal civilian employees. Even the low-end estimate (10,000+) represents a 2.5x multiplication of political appointees beyond the current ~4,000. The implementation mechanism: agencies identify "policy-influencing" roles (a category defined broadly enough to capture most positions requiring judgment rather than rote execution), then reclassify them.
Evaluation
Score = 3/10 (Persistent Impairment). Rationale: Reinstatement in 2025 plus the February 2026 refinement rule indicates sustained implementation, not symbolic gesture. This crosses into 3-4 territory (persistent impairment) because the mechanism is operational—agencies are reclassifying positions and the rule provides enforcement procedures. The difference from the October 2020 EO (scored 4/10 for design) is that this version is being implemented at scale. The score reflects institutional damage: converting career expertise into at-will loyalty positions removes the bureaucratic independence that historically slows or prevents unlawful executive actions. The civil service remains functional (not 0-2 range) but its structural role as a check is degraded. Timeline: reinstatement occurred early 2025, updated rule February 2026 per Federal Register and news reporting cited above.
Policy Language February 2026
The February 2026 Schedule Policy/Career implementation rule specifies that failure to "faithfully implement administration policies" constitutes grounds for dismissal, while stating that employees need not "personally or politically support the current President."
Evidence
  • Schedule Policy/Career implementation rule, Federal Register, February 2026
  • Analysis by Partnership for Public Service and Government Accountability Office on rule language
  • "Schedule F loyalty language," Brookings Institution analysis
Interpretation
This language attempts to thread a constitutional needle: "faithfully implement" sounds like the Take Care Clause (president's duty to execute laws faithfully), while "need not personally support" offers deniability against accusations of political litmus tests. The operational reality: if an employee can be dismissed for failing to implement a policy they consider unlawful, the "need not support" clause is illusory. Career civil servants historically provide friction against unlawful orders by citing statutes, regulations, and precedent. If that friction constitutes "failure to faithfully implement," expertise becomes insubordination. The mechanism converts professional judgment into political loyalty without using the word "loyalty."
Evaluation
Score = 3/10 (Persistent Impairment). Rationale: The language is scored separately from the Schedule Policy mechanism itself because it defines the enforcement criteria. "Faithfully implement" as dismissal grounds eliminates the distinction between disagreement and insubordination. This is persistent impairment (3-4 range) rather than strain (5-6) because it removes institutional capacity for lawful resistance—the ability of career staff to say "that directive violates statute X" without fear of dismissal. The rule's text ("faithfully implement") was published in the February 2026 Federal Register, documented in analyses by GAO, Partnership for Public Service, and Brookings cited above.

VI. The Judiciary: Shadow Docket and Active Cases

This section documents judicial responses to executive power consolidation, including procedural mechanisms that enable rapid implementation without full appellate review.

Procedural Mechanism 2015–present
In 2015, University of Chicago law professor William Baude coined the term "shadow docket" to describe the Supreme Court's practice of issuing significant emergency orders (stays, injunctions) without full briefing, oral argument, or signed opinions, enabling major policy changes to take effect before appellate review.
Evidence
  • William Baude, "Foreword: The Supreme Court's Shadow Docket," NYU Journal of Law & Liberty, Vol. 9 (2015)
  • "Shadow docket," Wikipedia, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow_docket
  • Justice Samuel Alito remarks on "shadow docket" term, Heritage Foundation event, 2021
Interpretation
The shadow docket's significance is procedural: it allows the Court to change policy outcomes without creating precedential reasoning. Emergency applications skip the deliberative process (briefing, argument, drafted opinions with dissents) that forces majority justices to articulate and defend their legal reasoning. When the Court grants a stay or injunction via shadow docket, lower courts receive a result (policy X is blocked/allowed) but no explanation of the legal principle. This enables executive actions to proceed while delaying the moment when the Court must publicly justify its reasoning, reducing democratic accountability and making it harder for future litigants to predict outcomes or for Congress to respond legislatively.
Evaluation
Score = 5/10 (Strained but Operative). Rationale: Shadow docket use doesn't eliminate judicial review (cases eventually reach full merits review), but it strains the transparency and reasoning requirements that make review meaningful. Justice Kagan's dissent in Whole Woman's Health v. Jackson (2021) called it "unreasoned, inconsistent, and impossible to defend"—the criticism isn't that the Court lacks authority for emergency orders, but that overuse of unreasoned orders degrades the judicial check. The judiciary remains operative (issuing rulings, hearing cases), but the shift toward outcome-without-explanation weakens the reasoning-based accountability that distinguishes law from power. Baude coined the term in 2015; increased use began notably during the Trump-era (2017-2021) and continued since.
Judicial Case 2025–pending
In 2025, the Supreme Court granted certiorari in Trump v. Slaughter, which challenges whether the President can fire Federal Trade Commission commissioners at will, potentially overturning Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935) and eliminating "for cause" protections for independent agencies including the Federal Reserve, SEC, FCC, and NLRB.
Evidence
  • Trump v. Slaughter docket, Supreme Court records, 2025
  • Humphrey's Executor v. United States, 295 U.S. 602 (1935)
  • SCOTUSBlog case analysis and docket tracking, scotusblog.com
  • Amicus briefs by Center for Renewing America and allied organizations, Supreme Court filings
Interpretation
Humphrey's Executor is the legal foundation for independent agencies—it established that Congress can insulate certain executive officers from at-will presidential removal. Overturning it would convert every "independent" agency into a direct presidential instrument. The implementation mechanism: if the Court rules the president can fire FTC commissioners at will, the same logic applies to the Federal Reserve (central bank independence), SEC (financial regulation), FCC (communications), NLRB (labor), and others. Independent agencies exist to depoliticize functions requiring expertise and long-term consistency. Eliminating their independence means decisions on interest rates, securities enforcement, broadcast licenses, and union elections become subject to presidential control and four-year political cycles. The Court's unusual step of granting cert before judgment (skipping the normal appellate process) plus the stay of the lower court ruling suggests urgency.
Evaluation
Pending — potential 3/10 (Persistent Impairment if Humphrey's overturned). Rationale: The case is pending as of February 2026, so current institutional health can't be scored on the outcome. But the trajectory is significant: the Court granted cert and stayed the lower court, signaling likely sympathy to the administration's position. If Humphrey's falls, the score would be 3/10 because eliminating independent agency protections removes a core structural check—central bank independence, for example, exists precisely to insulate monetary policy from short-term political pressure. The dissent by Justices Kagan, Sotomayor, and Jackson on the stay (3-justice dissent on a procedural order) indicates the Court's majority is willing to move quickly on this question. Timeline: cert granted 2025, argued or pending argument as of February 2026.
Judicial Case January 2026–pending
In January 2026, the Supreme Court accepted Trump v. Barbara, which challenges the interpretation of the Fourteenth Amendment's Citizenship Clause ("All persons born or naturalized in the United States, and subject to the jurisdiction thereof, are citizens"), seeking to overturn 128 years of precedent established in United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898) affirming birthright citizenship.
Evidence
  • Trump v. Barbara docket, Supreme Court, January 2026
  • United States v. Wong Kim Ark, 169 U.S. 649 (1898)
  • SCOTUSBlog analysis, scotusblog.com
  • Amicus briefs by Center for Renewing America, Claremont Institute, Article III Project
Interpretation
Wong Kim Ark (1898) settled that children born on U.S. soil are citizens regardless of parents' status, based on the Citizenship Clause's plain text: "all persons born... in the United States." The challenge argues "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" excludes children of undocumented immigrants. The mechanism sought: judicial reinterpretation enabling executive action (denying citizenship to children born in the U.S.). If the Court narrows Wong Kim Ark, future administrations can define citizenship by executive order rather than constitutional amendment. The significance isn't the immigration policy itself but the method—using judicial reinterpretation to grant executive authority over a constitutional question (who is a citizen) that was previously considered settled text.
Evaluation
Pending — potential 4/10 (Persistent Impairment if precedent overturned). Rationale: Like Trump v. Slaughter, this case is pending (accepted January 2026, not yet decided), so no current score applies. The potential impairment is in overturning 128 years of settled interpretation of constitutional text. Precedent stability is a structural check—it prevents each administration from redefining foundational questions. Birthright citizenship is constitutional (14th Amendment) rather than statutory, meaning change normally requires constitutional amendment (Article V: two-thirds of Congress, three-fourths of states). If the Court reinterprets the text to enable executive action, it transfers amendment-level authority to judicial-executive coordination. The amicus support from CRA (Vought's organization), Claremont, and Article III Project indicates coordinated effort to achieve through litigation what couldn't pass via Article V process.
Judicial Case 2025
In 2025, the administration federalized National Guard units and deployed them to Chicago; the Supreme Court denied the administration's request to stay a lower court order blocking aspects of the deployment, but the Court's order explicitly noted that the Insurrection Act could provide lawful authority, leaving that pathway open.

Interpretation
The Court's denial of a stay looks like a check (administration blocked), but the explicit note about the Insurrection Act is the signal. This is how judicial opinions function as implementation guides—the Court tells the executive branch which legal theory to use. SCOTUSBlog characterized this as the Court potentially providing "cover" for future invocation. The mechanism: domestic military deployment shifts from requiring actual insurrection to requiring only presidential invocation of the statutory authority.

This lands in 5-6 (strained) rather than 7-8 (robust) because the opinion's language provides the workaround. VIII. What the Framers Built — and Why

The Constitution of the United States is, at its core, an engineering document. Its primary engineering principle is the separation of powers—the deliberate distribution of governmental authority across three co-equal branches, each with the ability to check the others.

This is not inefficiency. It is not a bug. It is the core design feature, built by men who had lived under consolidated executive power and designed a system specifically to prevent its recurrence.

The Architecture

  • Three branches — Legislative (Article I), Executive (Article II), Judicial (Article III)—each granted specific, enumerated powers, each capable of checking the others.
  • Federalism — Power divided between federal and state governments, creating an additional layer of distributed authority (Tenth Amendment).
  • Bill of Rights — The first ten amendments function as constraints on government power, not grants of government authority. They enumerate things the government may not do.
  • Article V — The amendment process is deliberately difficult—requiring two-thirds of both houses of Congress and three-fourths of state legislatures—to prevent temporary political majorities from reshaping fundamental law.

“If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary. In framing a government which is to be administered by men over men, the great difficulty lies in this: you must first enable the government to control the governed; and in the next place oblige it to control itself.”

— Federalist No. 51

The system was designed by people who assumed that power would be abused. The question was not whether ambition would arise, but how to construct a system that could survive it. Every check, every friction point, every requirement for consensus across branches exists because the framers understood what happens when one person or faction accumulates unchecked authority.

Madison, in Federalist No. 47, was explicit: the accumulation of legislative, executive, and judicial power in the same hands is “the very definition of tyranny.” Hamilton, in Federalist No. 69, went point by point through every way the president was not a king. These were not theoretical exercises. They were the sales pitch to the American public for why this system of government should be adopted.

IX. The Citizen’s Duty

In the American system, sovereignty does not rest with the president, Congress, or the courts. It rests with the people. The republic’s health depends entirely on whether its sovereign—the citizenry—exercises its duty.

“Your first duties—first as regards importance—are, as I have already told you, towards Humanity. … But what can each of you, singly, do for the moral improvement and progress of Humanity? … The individual is too insignificant, and Humanity too vast.”

— Giuseppe Mazzini, The Duties of Man

Mazzini’s point was that rights without civic duty produce empty liberty. Paine’s purpose in writing Common Sense was to make the case so clearly that ordinary people could see through the mystifications of power. Thomas Jefferson wrote that “the spirit of resistance to government is so valuable on certain occasions, that I wish it to be always kept alive.”
